Project Overview

Why This Project, Why This Dataset

Olist is Brazil's largest department store marketplace, connecting small and medium-sized sellers across the country to major Brazilian retail channels through a single integrated platform. Sellers list products through Olist, and orders are fulfilled and shipped directly to customers across all 27 Brazilian states — making delivery logistics and regional demand two of the most commercially important variables in the business.

This dataset was chosen because it is real-world, multi-table relational data: over 99,000 orders, a genuine geographic spread across Brazil, and a two-year time horizon from September 2016 to October 2018. That combination of scale, structure, and messiness makes it a realistic stand-in for the kind of data a retail or e-commerce business analyst would actually be asked to model and report on.

The project set out to build a professional, two-page Power BI dashboard that answers five commercial business questions relevant to a retail or e-commerce analyst role — covering delivery performance, customer satisfaction, product category economics, and geographic concentration — using a proper star schema, Power Query cleaning, and DAX time-intelligence and ranking measures rather than flat, single-table reporting.

Business Questions

Five Questions the Dashboard Answers

Each question was defined before the model was built, and every KPI and chart on the dashboard maps back to one of these five.

  1. 1 How effective is our delivery speed compared with our estimated delivery time?
  2. 2 What is the impact of delivery speed on customer feedback?
  3. 3 Which product category is most frequently purchased and generates the most revenue?
  4. 4 Which product categories are most popular in each Brazilian state?
  5. 5 What is the geographic distribution of our customer base across Brazil?
Dataset & Methodology

How the Model Was Built

Dataset: Olist Brazilian E-Commerce Public Dataset — available free on Kaggle. Five of the nine available tables were used.

Table Role Rows
olist_orders_dataset Dimension 96,478 (filtered to delivered)
olist_customers_dataset Dimension 99,441
olist_products_dataset Dimension 32,951
olist_order_items_dataset Fact 112,650
olist_order_reviews_dataset Dimension 99,224

Data model: Star schema with Order Items as the central fact table, connected to four dimension tables via one-to-many relationships. A custom Date Table was built using M code in Power Query to enable time-intelligence DAX functions.

Data model diagram: the Order Items fact table sits at the centre, connected to Customers, Products, Order Reviews, and a custom Date Table via one-to-many relationships — a proper star schema rather than a flat, single-table model.

Tools used: Power BI Desktop, Power Query (M language), DAX.

Cleaning steps applied (Power Query)

  • Filtered the Orders table to delivered orders only
  • Removed unused columns across all tables
  • Converted date columns from Date/Time to Date type where appropriate
  • Replaced underscores with spaces in Portuguese product category names
  • Created a State Grouped calculated column to consolidate 17 smaller Brazilian states into a single "Other" category for the pie chart

Key DAX measures

  • Total Revenue = SUMX(Order Items, Price + Freight Value)
  • Total Orders Delivered = COUNTROWS(orders)
  • Average Delivery Speed = AVERAGEX(orders, DATEDIFF(Order Purchase Date, Delivery Date, DAY))
  • Average Estimated Delivery Speed = AVERAGEX(orders, DATEDIFF(Order Purchase Date, Estimated Delivery Date, DAY))
  • Average Customer Review Rating = AVERAGE(Order Reviews[Review Score])
  • Most Purchased Category = CONCATENATEX(TOPN(1, SUMMARIZE(...)))
  • Highest Revenue Product Category = CONCATENATEX(TOPN(1, SUMMARIZE(...)))
  • Total Orders by Category = COUNTROWS(Order Items)
  • Total Orders by State = COUNTROWS(Customers)
Key Findings

What the Data Actually Shows

One section per business question. Every figure below is confirmed from the dataset — nothing here is estimated or invented.

BQ1 · Delivery Speed vs Estimated Delivery Time

Orders arrive in half the time customers are told to expect

Olist's average actual delivery time is 12.5 days from order placement to customer receipt. The average estimated delivery time promised at checkout is 24.37 days — nearly double the actual performance. This roughly 12-day gap is consistent across every month in the dataset, with no seasonal exceptions, reflecting a deliberate strategy of conservative delivery promise-setting.

Commercial implication: this strategy protects satisfaction scores by making "on time or early" the norm, but a 24-day promise may be suppressing conversion among price-sensitive customers with faster alternatives. A recommended next step is analysing cart abandonment against delivery promise exposure to test whether shortening the estimated window is commercially justified, given the infrastructure clearly supports delivering in half the promised time.
BQ2 · Impact of Delivery Speed on Customer Feedback

Every extra day late costs review score, and the drop-off is steep

Delivery PerformanceAverage Review Score
Early (before estimated date)~4.3 / 5
On Time (on estimated date)~4.0 / 5
Late 1–3 Days~3.1 / 5
Super Late (>3 Days)~1.9 / 5

Orders delivered more than 3 days after the estimated date average just 1.9 out of 5 — a drop of over two full points versus on-time orders.

Analytical caveat: this is a strong correlation between delivery performance and review scores, not proof of causation. Product quality, seller responsiveness, and packaging aren't captured in this dataset and would need to be controlled for before treating this as a definitive causal conclusion.
BQ3 · Most Frequently Purchased vs Highest Revenue Category

Volume and revenue leaders are two different categories

The most frequently purchased category is cama mesa banho (bed, table & bath goods) — also the dominant category in São Paulo, Brazil's most populous state. The highest revenue-generating category, however, is beleza saude (health & beauty) — a different category entirely. Health and beauty products are purchased less often but at a higher average price point or in higher-margin configurations.

Commercial implication: a category strategy optimising for volume would reach a different conclusion than one optimising for revenue — the two shouldn't be conflated when prioritising merchandising or seller support.
BQ4 · Product Category Popularity by State

The national leader isn't the leader everywhere

Regional variation in purchasing preferences is confirmed. While cama mesa banho dominates nationally by order volume, individual states diverge — Paraná (PR), for example, shows moveis decoracao (furniture & home decoration) as its top category instead of the national leader. São Paulo (SP) alone generates 41.98% of all orders and $5.77M of the total $15.84M revenue, and the top three states — SP, RJ, and MG — together account for roughly 70% of total order volume.

BQ5 · Geographic Distribution of Customer Base

The business is heavily concentrated in Brazil's southeast

São Paulo alone accounts for 41.98% of all orders, and the top 10 states by order volume account for approximately 90.5% of all activity — the remaining 17 states collectively contribute just 9.51%.

Commercial implication: this concentration is both a risk and an opportunity — the business is heavily dependent on a single region, but most of Brazil's geographic area and population remains relatively untapped from an e-commerce penetration standpoint.

Limitations & Next Steps

What This Doesn't Show — And What's Next

Limitations

  • Product names aren't available — analysis is at category level only, not individual SKU level.
  • Customer IDs are anonymised and unique per order rather than per person, so true customer lifetime value or repeat-purchase analysis isn't possible.
  • Review comments are in Portuguese and predominantly absent (over 58% of rows have no comment), making sentiment analysis unfeasible without NLP tooling.
  • The BQ2 finding establishes correlation between delivery performance and review scores, not causation — additional variables would need to be controlled for.

Recommended Next Steps

  • Integrate cart abandonment data to test whether the 12-day delivery promise gap suppresses conversion.
  • Add seller-level data to investigate whether late deliveries concentrate among specific sellers or product categories.
  • Explore seasonal trends more granularly using the full date dimension (Quarter, Day of Week) already built into the model.
  • Build a seller performance page as a third dashboard tab, reloading the order items seller_id column removed during cleaning.
